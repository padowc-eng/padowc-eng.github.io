ATLAS Passive Knee Exoskeleton

A senior design entrepreneurship project focused on a passive knee-assist device for hiking, knee strain reduction, mechanical design, prototyping, and product development.

Project overview

ATLAS was a senior design entrepreneurship project focused on developing a passive knee exoskeleton for hikers and active users. The design goal was to create a mechanical assist system that could reduce knee strain without relying on motors, batteries, or a complicated control system. 

// design direction
project: "passive knee exoskeleton"
target_user: "hikers / active users"
main_constraint: "useful assist without making the system bulky or complex"

Problem being solved

Hiking, especially downhill or over long distances, can create repeated knee loading. The project explored whether a passive mechanical system could support knee extension and reduce strain while still feeling practical to wear.

  • Reduce knee strain during repeated hiking motion.
  • Keep the design lightweight and mechanically simple.
  • Avoid powered components unless absolutely necessary.
  • Balance assist behavior, comfort, cost, and manufacturability.

My role

I worked as a design lead and helped move the project from an open-ended concept toward a prototype direction. The work was not just CAD. It required deciding what the mechanism should do, what constraints mattered, and what design path was realistic.

  • Led mechanical concept development and prototype direction.
  • Worked through mechanism layout, assist behavior, and design tradeoffs.
  • Helped define requirements around weight, adjustability, cost, and usability.
  • Presented design decisions through reviews and entrepreneurship-focused work.

Engineering work

Mechanism thinking

The concept focused on passive assistance during knee motion. The design needed to store and return energy in a useful way while avoiding a system that would fight the user or become too complicated to wear.

Prototype constraints

The design had to be judged against real constraints: weight, fit, comfort, cost, durability, manufacturability, and whether a user would actually want to use it outdoors.

Product direction

Because this was also an entrepreneurship-focused project, the work included more than the technical mechanism. It also required thinking about user need, market fit, early cost targets, and what a realistic first prototype could prove.
